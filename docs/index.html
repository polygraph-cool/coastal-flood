Changes in the climate since the 1980s have put a growing share of New Jersey’s coastal communities at risk.

Tens of thousands of homes are newly threatened by flooding and hurricane winds.

But an even greater risk is on the horizon. If carbon pollution continues unchecked, New Jersey could face $1.9-$4.3 billion in additional annual damages due to hurricanes compared to 1980.

Part 1

A Rising Tide

New Jersey is already feeling the effects of sea level rise. The sea level around Atlantic City, for instance, has risen more than 6.4 inches since 1980. On average, sea levels around the state have risen six inches in this time period, compared to the global average rise of only three inches.

Sea level rise can devastate coastal habitats, causing destructive erosion, wetland flooding, and soil contamination. As the coast erodes and waters rise, so do the number of properties regularly swamped by tidal flooding.

Part 2

Hurricanes

Though Sandy was only a Category 1 storm, it delivered storm surge most commonly seen during Category 4 hurricanes along the North Jersey shore.

Sandy’s flooding almost completely inundated barrier islands in Ocean County, with storm surge and large Atlantic Ocean waves encountering rising waters from back bays such as Barnegat Bay and Little Egg Harbor.

Rising sea levels mean that even weaker storms can bring destructive water levels and cause substantial property damage.

In fact, for a hurricane that makes landfall in New Jersey, storm surge appears to be the greatest threat to life and property.

Here are all the properties currently at risk of being inundated by storm surge flooding during the next hurricane to strike New Jersey. There are about 11,000 properties in total, housing more than 26,000 people.

But by 2050, the number of properties at risk could nearly triple. That means more than 50,000 residents could be newly exposed to the risk of powerful hurricane-related flooding.

By definition, hurricane winds are at least 74 miles-per-hour. But the maximum wind speed at hurricane landfall is not the sole factor in explaining property damage from a storm.

Other characteristics, like wind duration, changes in direction, and the storm’s path are also known to drive damage. Factors like building density, building age, and wind-borne debris also impact the number of structures at risk.

Changes in the climate over the past 30 years mean more properties are threatened by hurricane-force winds.

The number of properties at risk has more than quadrupled since the 1980s.

Forecasts out to 2050 suggest that the number of properties at risk of damage by hurricane winds will continue to increase.

Part 3

What Will It Cost?

New Jersey real estate is already more vulnerable than it was in the 1980s. Storms that previously would have occurred only once per century are now happening every 13 years.

In other words, such a storm is likely to occur twice during the duration of an average 30-year mortgage.

In absolute terms, Hudson County faces the greatest risk with likely annual damages in 2020 estimated to be between $301 million and $663 million higher than they would have been in the climate of the 1980s.

Bergen County, the most populous in the state, is likely to see $148-$254 million in additional annual storm damages due to higher sea levels and changes in hurricane activity since the 1980s.

The situation will only become worse in the coming decades. New Jersey is on course for annual average losses due to hurricanes of $1.9-$4.3 billion greater than the 1980s.

Sixty years of climate change is likely to increase damages by $877 million to $2.1 billion from for Hudson County by 2050, roughly 4% of projected county-level GDP in that time period.
